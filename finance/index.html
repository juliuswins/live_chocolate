YOUR SAVINGS ACCOUNT

Let's say you have an Australian bank account. It's convenient.

It has a debit card attached to your spending account, that you can use to freely spend your money.

You have a savings account that pays you monthly interest on money you hold in it.

 

Banks need to keep a minimum of 10% of your capital as collateral.

The rest, they generally loan out or invest - and for this, they are paying your savings account a variable interest rate based on their return, or a fixed amount.

The rate is generally around 4-6% in Australia as of early 2024.

 

Effectively, you are investing in your bank and getting a return. The bank is unlikely to liquidate, and you are very likely to keep the money you have.

 

But you and your bank are invested in the Australian dollar, which is in ever-increasing supply and therefore loses its value of what it can buy over time - known commonly as inflation.

 

REAL TALK

Consider real return - Which is the REAL value of your money. Even if the money is going up, if the rate that money is inflating (increased supply of cash in the market) faster than you are making money, you're still losing wealth.

Why? Because of that investment in the Australian dollar, and because companies like to increase their prices to generate more profits for their investors.

INVESTING POCKETS OF MONEY.

What if you considered each asset or investment like a small bank account?

Instead of keeping that sum of money saved in a bank account, it's in a greater market where it will grow at a variable rate each day the stock market is open.

The more days you keep it in this market, the better, provided you're investing in the right areas. After all, if you're investing in something that is going to increase in demand, it's going to eventually increase in price.

 

So, we should make a Portfolio that will be better than our 6% savings account, right?

(Exhibit 3: Our simple but relatively diverse portfolio.)

This simple portfolio contains:

-Some money in our savings account for easy access to money.

-A cheap vanguard fund of the entire world stock market.

-A cheap vanguard fund of the top 500 companies on the New York Stock Exchange.

-A fund that holds Bitcoin.

- A fund that holds Gold.

-A chosen and researched company (Google) we expect will continue to grow at a reasonable rate.

 

FUNDING STRATEGY VS INDIVIDUAL COMPANIES

For your investment in a company or a fund to grow, its enterprise value (also known as market capitalization or market value) must increase. If a company or fund is worth $1 billion, then it would need $10,000,000 to be invested in the company for its value to increase 1%.

A fund is generally more stable and is an effective way to buy into an assortment of companies of a particular market or industry, where the most profitable companies will balance out and hopefully outperform the lagging companies to capture a movement that overall demonstrates that overall market or sector's sentiment.

If you think RARE EARTH is going to be a growing sector, you could buy REMX on the New York Stock Exchange and you would essentially have ownership split between several companies mining for Rare Earth, and the growth of the sector would reflect through many of these companies.

WHAT DOES AVERAGE DOWN MEAN THOUGH?

Institutions often will buy or sell their larger positions in parts for 2 reasons: so that it is less obvious what their moves are, and so that they can buy and sell in a more reasonable, averaged out price. We can do this too on a smaller scale with the bit of money we have.

Because the market has crashed, sentiment is low. The market values things cheaper than it once did. Will Gold disappear, is Bitcoin no longer existent, does America's stock market still function? If they're going to eventually come back, you have a chance to buy more at a discount.

The market crashing has made Gold outperform, and your cash was now useful to hold aside; You can use your cash and sell some profits from your Gold position to re-adjust your portfolio.

This is why timing can be important in the market, but if you wait long enough you won't need to worry as long as you're placing money in the right place.

If you bought 10 shares of the total market for $100 per share, then it fell to $50, then you bought 10 more for $50, you have bought 20 shares for $75 per share. This means that once the total market is worth $100 per share again, you are at a profit now at your original entry point.

 

UNDERSTAND COMPOUND INTEREST

The longer you hold assets, the more likely you will see them eventually grow to increasingly large sizes.

The amount it increases works in percentages, not dollars per hour. Thus, the more you have in there, the more it can become. Eventually, it can grow big enough to make more money for you than your human physical or mental effort ever could.

Because of this, you can increase the rate of this growth by adding money regularly. Whether the price is going up or down, putting more in will equate to more risk and thus, more reward.

This calculator will give you an idea of the impact:

https://moneysmart.gov.au/budgeting/compound-interest-calculator

You will see that adding $50 every week is adding another $2,600 into your portfolio of 'pockets' to invest.
